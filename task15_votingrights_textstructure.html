📖 Reading Passage

Women's Suffrage: The Right to Vote

For over 100 years after America was founded, women couldn't vote. Laws said only men could vote. Women also couldn't own property if they were married. Husbands owned everything. Women couldn't be lawyers, doctors, or many other jobs. In 1848, a group of women met in Seneca Falls, New York. They wrote a document called the Declaration of Sentiments. It said women were equal to men and deserved equal rights. The first right they demanded was the right to votechoose leaders through elections.

For 72 years, women advocatedargued for; fought for for voting rights. Susan B. Anthony was arrested for voting illegally. Elizabeth Cady Stanton gave speeches. Women marched in parades. Some were put in jail. But they didn't give up. In 1920, the 19th Amendment was finally passed. It said women had the right to vote. After 72 years of fighting, women could finally have a say in their government.

But winning the right to vote was just one victory. Women still fought for equal pay, equal jobs, and respect. The struggle for women's rights didn't end in 1920. It continues today. Understanding women's suffrage teaches us that change takes time, hard work, and never giving up on what's right.
